PART I: Theoretical Foundations (20 min)

"For decades, finance theory rested on a beautiful lie: that humans are rational calculators who always maximize utility. Your fountain pen auction just proved otherwise. Let's understand why."

1. Classical Finance Theory: The Rational Foundation (5 min)

The Holy Trinity of Traditional Finance

A. Efficient Market Hypothesis (EMH) - Fama, 1970
  • Core claim: Prices fully reflect all available information
  • Implication: You cannot consistently beat the market
  • Assumption: Rational investors instantly arbitrage away mispricings
  • Indian context: If EMH held perfectly, why do mutual fund managers exist?
B. Rational Actor Model

Assumptions:

  • Perfect information processing
  • Stable, consistent preferences
  • Maximization of expected utility
  • No cognitive limitations

Problem: "This describes Vulcans, not humans"

C. Expected Utility Theory (von Neumann & Morgenstern, 1944)
  • People maximize expected value weighted by probability
  • Formula: E(U) = Œ£ p(x) √ó U(x)
  • Prediction: Risk attitudes are consistent and stable
  • Reality check: Why do people buy lottery tickets AND insurance?

Transition: "But if we're all rational maximizers, why did some of you just bid ‚Çπ800 for a ‚Çπ200 pen simply because your phone number was higher? Classical theory has no answer. Behavioral finance does."

We overvalue what we already own

Famous mug experiment:

  • Students GIVEN mugs: Won't sell for less than ‚Çπ400
  • Students WITHOUT mugs: Won't buy for more than ‚Çπ150

PART II: Core Biases Taxonomy (15 min)

After each bias: "Raise hands if you've done this with YOUR money"

‚öì BIAS 1: ANCHORING

Definition

The tendency to rely heavily on the first piece of information (the "anchor") when making decisions, even when that anchor is arbitrary or irrelevant.

Financial Manifestation

  • IPO pricing: First day listing price becomes psychological anchor
  • Stock "52-week high": Investors wait for stock to return to previous peak before selling
  • Negotiation: First offer in salary/vendor discussions disproportionately influences final agreement

Indian Example: Reliance Power IPO (2008)

  • Issue price: ‚Çπ450 per share
  • Massive oversubscription: 70x retail, 23x overall
  • Market debut: ‚Çπ372 (17% below anchor)
  • Current reality: Trading around ‚Çπ25-30 (2024)
  • Anchoring tragedy: Investors held waiting for "return to ‚Çπ450" for 15+ years

Personal Example: "You bought HDFC Bank at ‚Çπ1,800. It's now ‚Çπ1,400. You think, 'I'll sell when it gets back to ‚Çπ1,800.' The ‚Çπ1,800 is an anchor‚Äîit has no bearing on the company's future prospects."

‚úì BIAS 2: CONFIRMATION BIAS

Definition

The tendency to search for, interpret, favor, and recall information that confirms pre-existing beliefs while giving disproportionately less consideration to alternative possibilities.

Financial Manifestation

  • Selective research: Only reading bullish reports on stocks you own
  • Holding losers: Finding reasons why "the market is wrong"
  • Echo chambers: Following only analysts who agree with your thesis

Indian Example: "My Mutual Fund Will Bounce Back"

Scenario:

  • Investor holds sector fund (e.g., infrastructure) down 40%
  • Reads only articles about "infrastructure boom coming"
  • Ignores: Debt problems, project delays, management issues
  • Confirmation bias + sunk cost fallacy = Extended losses

Impact: Average individual investor underperforms index by 3-4% annually (Barber & Odean)

üí™ BIAS 3: OVERCONFIDENCE

Definition

Systematic overestimation of one's own abilities, knowledge, or predictions relative to objective standards or the abilities of others.

Indian Example: Day Trading Losses

SEBI Study (2023):

  • 89% of individual equity traders LOST money over FY19-FY22
  • Average loss: ‚Çπ1.1 lakh per trader
  • Only 11% profitable (and most barely break even after costs)
  • Volume: Daily trading by individuals increased 400% during pandemic

The Irony:

Question to audience: "How many think they'd be in the profitable 11%?"

Prediction: Most hands will rise (proving overconfidence in real-time)

üò∞ BIAS 4: LOSS AVERSION (Disposition Effect)

Definition

The tendency to feel the pain of losses roughly 2-2.5 times more intensely than the pleasure of equivalent gains, leading to risk-seeking behavior when facing losses.

Financial Manifestation: The Disposition Effect

  • Sell winners too early: Lock in gains to "feel good"
  • Hold losers too long: Avoid realizing loss (mental accounting)
  • Tax inefficiency: Exactly backward from optimal tax strategy
  • Result: Portfolios full of underperformers, winners cashed out

Indian Example: NSE Disposition Effect

Odean (1998) replicated in India by Barber et al. (2007):

  • Indian retail investors sold winners 50% more readily than losers
  • Stocks sold (winners): Subsequent 1-month return = +0.8%
  • Stocks held (losers): Subsequent 1-month return = -1.2%
  • Net cost: 2% per month = 24% annualized wealth destruction

üë• BIAS 5: HERD BEHAVIOR

Definition

The tendency to mimic the actions of a larger group, whether rational or irrational, driven by social proof, FOMO (fear of missing out), and informational cascades.

Indian Example: 2008 Crisis ‚Üí 2017 Crypto Boom

2008 Market Crash:
  • Sensex peak: 21,000 (Jan 2008)
  • Sensex bottom: 8,000 (Mar 2009) ‚Üí 62% drop
  • Herd panic: Redemption pressures forced mutual funds to sell
  • Retail exodus: SIP discontinuations peaked when markets were cheapest
2017-2018 Crypto Mania:
  • Bitcoin: ‚Çπ4.5L (Jan 2017) ‚Üí ‚Çπ14L (Dec 2017) ‚Üí ‚Çπ2.5L (Dec 2018)
  • Herd entry: Relatives asking "How to buy Bitcoin?" at peak
  • WhatsApp groups: "10x in 3 months!" messages proliferated
  • Outcome: 70-80% losses for late entrants

üìÖ BIAS 6: RECENCY BIAS

Definition

The tendency to weigh recent events more heavily than earlier events, assuming that recent trends will continue indefinitely (extrapolation error).

Indian Example: Mutual Fund Inflows Lag Performance

Pattern (Repeats Every Cycle):

  1. Year 1-2: Small-cap fund delivers 40% returns
  2. Year 3: Media coverage, awards ‚Üí MASSIVE inflows
  3. Year 4: Fund now too big, small-cap cycle ends ‚Üí Underperforms
  4. Result: Late investors buy at peak, suffer subsequent losses

AMFI Statistics (2020-2021):

  • Sectoral/thematic fund inflows: ‚Çπ18,000 crores
  • Period: AFTER tech/pharma rally was mature
  • Subsequent 18 months: Most funds gave back 30-40% gains

Synthesis: The Bias Interaction Web

How Biases Compound

Scenario: Tech stock rally (2020-2021)

  1. Recency bias: "Tech always goes up" (based on 18 months)
  2. Herd behavior: Everyone buying Zomato, Nykaa IPOs
  3. Overconfidence: "I know tech is the future"
  4. Confirmation bias: Only reading bullish tech articles
  5. Anchoring: Zomato IPO price = ‚Çπ76 becomes reference
  6. Loss aversion: Won't sell at ‚Çπ50 because "I paid ‚Çπ76"

Result: Multi-bias trap leading to sustained losses
